1. **agent-first (bullish deployment) ↔ enterprise-ai-reality (brutal bench rate):** Both are true simultaneously. They operate at different time-horizons — direction vs current-rate — but a reader encountering them without a bridge will see contradiction. R9 must stitch this explicitly.

## 3. Context-not-contradiction (different abstraction layers)

### **`agent-first` ↔ `spec-first-taste` / `reversibility-over-consequences`**

Agent-first is bullish on deployment. Spec-first-taste says invest in preparation before you run the agent. Reversibility-over-consequences says keep agents away from decisions that cannot be evaluated programmatically. These look like they are arguing against each other but they operate at different architectural layers.

Agent-first is a structural bet about the direction of computing — agents are the new substrate, build for them. Spec-first-taste is a craft practice at the builder's desk — spec well before invoking any tool, including agentic ones. Reversibility-over-consequences is an authorization constraint at the deployment layer — not IF to deploy agents, but WHICH decisions they get to make alone. All three can be held simultaneously: yes to agentic architecture, yes to speccing before building, yes to constraining agent decision-scope to programmatically-evaluable choices.

The agent-first manifesto (2025-06-20) includes both the bullish direction claim AND the serving-lens-first-building-lens-second framing. The 2026-01-06 reversibility post follows agent-first, doesn't contradict it. They are three instruments in the same ensemble: strategy, craft, authorization.

R9 note: these three themes cross-link naturally but the cross-link text must be precise. "Context-not-contradiction" is the operative label, not "these agree."

Evidence: 2025-06-20 (agent-first + serving-lens together); 2026-01-06 `urn:li:activity:7414150680820547584` (reversibility as deployment constraint, not anti-deployment); 2026-04-09 (spec-first as craft practice, not skepticism about agentic direction).

These share a single canonical post (2019-12-04 driver/mechanic/engineer taxonomy) and a shared concern (surface-tool-fluency will be automated). They can look like the same belief, but they operate at different question-levels.

The cross-link between these pages should be explicit that they answer different questions, not that they say the same thing.

## 6. Tension with industry consensus

### **Agam: NFRs cause 55% of production failures / Industry consensus: model quality is the bottleneck**

Agam's stance (2024-03-08 Collab Article, citing Standish Chaos Report 2023): 55% of software production issues are due to non-functional requirements — performance, security, scalability, usability. By implication, the AI project that fails does not fail on model capability; it fails on the boring stuff that surrounds the model.

Prevailing consensus: the conversation in most AI teams and in most AI media circa 2024-2026 centers on model quality (better model = better product), benchmarks, and inference optimization. NFRs are treated as engineering housekeeping delegated to ops/QA, not as PM primary work.

Why this matters for the wiki reader: an AI PM who accepts the consensus will underfund NFR specification and over-invest in model selection. Agam's framing reverses the priority: nail NFRs at PRD-time, treat model selection as a secondary variable. The 10-to-2 production rate is largely explained by NFR failure, not model failure. This is an actionable disagreement with field consensus.

Note: the 55% Standish number requires verification before wiki publication (flagged as Open Q in R6 pm-taste and enterprise-ai-reality drafts). If unverifiable, reframe as dispositional ("NFRs are the dominant failure mode") without the specific statistic.

Evidence: 2024-03-08 Collab Article (`urn:li:activity:7171698939044196352`); 2024-09-04 nine-takeaways post.

### **Agam: data moat is pipeline architecture, not corpus size / Industry consensus: more data wins**

Agam's stance (2024-06-07 Collab Article, 2024-12-04 moats post): "Having data is one thing; having AI-ready data is where the value lies."
